Christmas markets and gothic castles: The best things to do in Lbeck this winter

June 13, 2024
blog

A magical atmosphere and amazing attractions make the Christmas capital of northern Germany an ideal escape from the hustle and bustle back home.

Visitors can wind back the clock and enjoy the craftsmanship and rituals of the past in a truly enchanting setting.

Lübeck boasts nine yuletide markets bursting with delicious foods, gifts and entertainment.

The Lübeck Christmas Market, which has been running since 1648, features more than 400 merchants selling all kinds of handmade toys, decorations, quality wooden ornaments, gingerbread and hot spiced wine. And against the gothic backdrop of St Mary’s church, known as the Marienkirche, coppersmiths, silversmiths and other craftsmen sell their wares.

As night falls, wood snaps and crackles in huge fire drums as jugglers and musicians in period costume perform. Nearby, fantasy and reality meet at Lübeck’s Fairy Tale Forest which has been a popular festive attraction for both locals and tourists for 30 years.

Here, children and big kids are enchanted by 500 illuminated fir trees and 21 stalls recreating tales of the Brothers Grimm.

An excellent craft market in the vaults of the old Holy Spirit Hospital is the perfect hidden treasure trove, housing 50 craftspeople from Germany, Scandinavia, the Baltic States and Israel showcasing their most beautiful works. Keep an eye on the time as it’s easy to get sidetracked by even more Christmas market stalls on the Rathausmarkt next to the town hall, at St Peter’s Church, by the Heiligen-Geist Hospital and in Koberg Square.

Lübeck is also known for its love affair with marzipan, which is a speciality at Café Niederegger in the heart of the old town. More than 300 delicacies and all kinds of coffee are available at this chic meeting place. A personal favourite is the Black Forest cherry gateau with marzipan cherries.

A mini museum on the second floor tells the story of marzipan from its oriental roots in the 12th Century through to its arrival in Lübeck. During the tour you’ll meet 12 life-size marzipan models of the people who played a role in the history of the Niederegger brand. A family business, Niederegger has been creating and selling top quality marzipan since 1806.

Ditching my glad rags to don overalls and a red hair net I explored the company’s factory where 700 staff work to produce marzipan bars, fruits and models of buildings found in the town. The firm uses a strictlyguarded recipe handed down through two centuries, which includes almonds, sugar and a mystery ingredient similar to rose-water.

A popular place to dine is the Schiffergesellschaft, a modern restaurant located in the historic seafarers’ guildhall, which dates back to 1535.

Ships’ lanterns, model ships and revolving Chinese-style silhouette lamps dangle from the beamed ceiling of the wood-lined dining room. Here waiting staff suggest old regional specialties such as wiener schnitzel.

But nothing screams history more than the entrance to the old town at the Holstentor Gate, which is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Leaning at an odd angle and with its sunken south tower, the 15th Century fortifi cation symbolised Lübeck’s status as a free imperial city.

The Holstentor Gate is within walking distance of the Radisson Blu Senator Hotel, Lübeck, which was the perfect place to rest after my wanders through the cobbled streets of the city.

The hotel overlooks the picturesque River Trave, offering a location that is both convenient and scenic. There’s lots to learn about Lübeck’s rich past and Christmas is a great time to visit, buy some handmade gifts and enjoy the festive spirit.
